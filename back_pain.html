BACK PAIN (Acute or Chronic)

Back pain is usually caused by an injury to the muscles or ligaments of the spine. Sometimes the disks that separate each bone in the spine may bulge and cause pain by pressing on a nearby nerve. Back pain may appear after a sudden twisting/bending force (such as in a car accident), after a simple awkward movement, or lifting something heavy with poor body positioning. In either case, muscle spasm is often present and adds to the pain.

Acute back pain usually gets better in one to two weeks. Back pain related to disk disease, arthritis in the spinal joints or spinal stenosis (narrowing of the spinal canal) can become chronic and last for months or years.

Unless you had a physical injury (for example, a car accident or fall) X-rays are usually not ordered for the initial evaluation of back pain. If pain continue and does not respond to medical treatment, x-rays and other tests may be performed at a later time.

Home Care:

  1. You should rest as needed. But as soon as possible, begin sitting or walking to avoid problems with prolonged bed rest (muscle weakness, worsening of back stiffness and pain, blood clots in legs).
  2. When in bed, try to find a position of comfort. A firm mattress is best. Try lying flat on your back with pillows under your knees. You can also try lying on your side with your knees bent up towards your chest and a pillow between your knees.
  3. Avoid prolong sitting. This puts more stress on the lower back than standing or walking.
  4. During the first two days after injury, apply an ICE PACK to the painful area for 20 minutes every 2-4 hours. This will reduce swelling and pain. HEAT (hot shower, hot bath or heating pad) works well for muscle spasms. You can start with ice, then switch to heat after two days. Some patients feel best alternating ice and heat treatments. Use the one method that feels best for you.
  5. You may use acetaminophen (Tylenol) 650mg-1000mg every 6 hours or ibuprofen (Motrin, Advil) 600mg every 6-8 hours with food to control pain, if you are able to take these medications. [NOTES: If you have chronic liver or kidney disease or ever had a stomach ulcer or GI bleeding, talk with your doctor before using these medications.]
  6. Be aware of safe lifting methods and do not lift anything over 15 pounds until all the pain is gone.

Follow Up: with your primary care provider if your symptoms do not start to inprove after one week. Your PCP may consider using physical therapy to help you recover.

Get Prompt Medical Attention if any of the following occurs:

  1. Pain becomes worse or spreads to your legs
  2. Weakness or numbness in one or both legs
  3. Loss of bowel or bladder control
  4. Numbness in the groin or genital area
